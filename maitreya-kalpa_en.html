This text is composed by a lay practitioner, attempting to glimpse the true meaning of the Tathāgata's teachings based on sutra logic and patriarchal commentaries. Should there be any omissions or errors, they stem from my personal intellectual limits. May the Three Jewels bless us, and may fellow practitioners share in this reflection.

Descent of Maitreya ． Endless Succession

I. Śākyamuni's Prophecy & The Three Assemblies
According to the *Maitreya Vyākaraṇa Sūtra* and other Mahayana scriptures, Śākyamuni Buddha profoundly entrusted the great vow of Anuttarā Samyaksaṃbodhi to Mahāsattva Maitreya amidst the Dharma assembly. Bodhisattva Maitreya currently resides in the inner court of Tuṣita Heaven as the Buddha-to-be. When conditions ripen, he shall descend to the Sahā world and attain enlightenment beneath the towering Naga-puspa (Dragon Flower) tree, convening three great assemblies to deliver sentient beings:

First Assembly: Delivering 96 billion of those who observed the five precepts during Śākyamuni's Dharma era.
Second Assembly: Delivering 94 billion of those who took the three refuges.
Third Assembly: Delivering 92 billion of those who even merely chanted 'Namo Buddha'.

This prophecy is perhaps not merely a succession of the Dharma lineage, but a compassionate covenant of all Buddhas, never abandoning sentient beings, generating life after life in endless continuity.
II. Foundation of Sukhavati & Virtues of Samantabhadra
In the past, Master Xuanzang, a grandmaster of the Yogācāra school, possessed a clear consciousness and an adamantine will, which perhaps enabled him to ascend directly to Tuṣita. However, the minds of ordinary beings are often as fragile as glass. Without the foundational embrace of Amitābha’s original vows, drifting in the ocean of karma might be like a lone boat braving massive waves; a slight misstep could lead one to drift endlessly amidst the illusion of rebirth.

The scriptures metaphorically indicate that Sukhavati (Ultimate Bliss) might not be the final destination for practitioners to rest and indulge. Being born in the Pure Land and attaining Avaivartika (non-retrogression) seems to serve the purpose of allowing practitioners to steadily accumulate Bodhi provisions. If we contemplate the profound meaning of Amitābha’s 22nd Vow, Bodhisattvas born in that land will ultimately reach the stage of becoming a Buddha in one lifetime; yet, if they wish to transcend ordinary limits, they may don the armor of great vows, return to the Sahā world, and cultivate the virtues of Samantabhadra. This perhaps implies that Sukhavati is an exalted realm meant to protect and nurture ordinary beings to practice the Bodhisattva path and return to the Sahā world.
III. The Eternal Dharmakaya & Returning by Vows
Observing the Dharma realm, the Reward and Transformation bodies (Sambhogakāya and Nirmāṇakāya) all seem to manifest signs of arising and ceasing; perhaps only the pure Dharmakāya can remain eternal and unfading. According to the profound mysteries revealed in the *Vyākaraṇa of Avalokiteśvara Sūtra*, although the lifespan of Amitābha Buddha in Sukhavati is inconceivable, when his conditions for transformation conclude, he too might manifest Parinirvāṇa. Countless kalpas after the Buddha’s nirvana, when the True Dharma eventually fades, Bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara shall attain perfect enlightenment under the seven-jeweled Bodhi tree, bearing the title 'Samantaprabhāsa-guṇaparvatarāja Tathāgata'.

This continuity within impermanence perhaps narrates the profound significance of the Dharma lineage’s transmission. Reflecting deeply upon this, our reliance on Amitābha's vows to attain a non-retrogressive body might simply be so that, when conditions ripen, we can more gracefully attend Bodhisattva Maitreya’s three assemblies.
IV. Bhadrakalpa's 1000 Buddhas & Endless Succession
According to the *Bhadrakalpika Sūtra*, the spacetime we reside in is known as the Bhadrakalpa. During this vast kalpa, one thousand Buddhas shall sequentially descend to the Sahā world, perhaps precisely to continuously deliver sentient beings without interruption.

The Dharma is like an eternal beacon in the universe. In the 9th minor kalpa, four Buddhas arise; in the 10th, Maitreya descends; and in the 15th, 994 Buddhas successively manifest, until the 20th minor kalpa with Rucika Buddha, which seems to complete the count of the thousand Buddhas.

Today, relying on the foundation of Sukhavati in hopes of attaining a non-retrogressive body, is perhaps preparing for this immense cosmic covenant. When future conditions ripen, we might manifest as specks of dust to return by vows amidst this endless succession of a thousand Buddhas, and perhaps even in the future bounds, bloom our own golden lotuses of Buddhahood.
