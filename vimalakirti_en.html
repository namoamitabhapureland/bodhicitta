Any omissions or errors are solely due to the limitations of my own understanding. May the Three Jewels bestow their blessings, and may fellow practitioners kindly bear witness.

Vimalakirti Sutra · The Non-Dual Gate

I. Dawn of Mahayana · The Lay Catalyst
Tracing back to the early sectarian monastic schools, the sudden emergence of Mahayana texts and doctrines perhaps carried the profound vows of lay practitioners within the red dust. The monumental treatises of great beings like Asvaghosa and Nagarjuna could align flawlessly with the Tathagata's true meaning not merely through worldly intellect. Ancient masters hinted that such beings were perhaps Bodhisattvas returning by vow, manifesting in specific eras to bridge liberation and worldly compassion. This suggests that the Dharma has no fixed form; anyone, monastic or lay, who aligns with the Buddha's intent can turn the wheel of Mahayana.
II. Entering the Red Dust · Pure Land of Mind
The Vimalakirti Sutra states: "When the mind is pure, the Buddha land is pure." This perhaps reveals that the Pure Land is not in a distant realm, but within the mind itself. Residing in this world of five impurities, living as a householder with a family might seem contrary to liberation. Yet, maintaining awareness amidst the chaos of the red dust—practicing the Dharma without abandoning the world—is perhaps the most resilient foundation of the Bodhisattva path.
III. Mustard Seed · The Infinite Vessel
Vimalakirti's square room, seemingly tiny, effortlessly contained the 32,000 enormous lion thrones of the Buddha Merupradiparaja. Such mythical phenomena were perhaps intended to shatter the rigid human attachment to "physical space and size." When we are no longer confined by the smallness of our physical bodies, the inner capacity of a pure mind can be as vast as the universe, embracing the majesty of all Dharma realms.
IV. Affliction and Bodhi · The Non-Dual Gate
People often view affliction and Bodhi, birth-and-death and Nirvana, as opposing extremes. Yet, the "Non-Dual Gate" perhaps suggests that these seemingly conflicting energies are inherently of the same emptiness. With a biased perspective, one only sees the chaos of the karmic ocean; but if one centralizes their view and enters non-duality, the restless dust of affliction and the pure light of Bodhi seemingly overlap in the present moment, transforming into a perfect lotus.
V. Turning to the Great · The One Vehicle
While early teachings opened the door to self-liberation through the Four Noble Truths, the aspiration of Mahayana cannot stagnate at the Arhat's solitary Nirvana. Bodhisattvas must turn from the small to the great, enduring kalpas in Samsara to save sentient beings, awaiting the prophecy of Buddhahood. The ultimate destination of all these diverse practices is perhaps guiding towards the Pure Land and the "One Buddha Vehicle." Every responsibility we shoulder in the red dust today might be the provision for our future Buddhahood.
VI. The 13th Tier · Plucking Lotus from the Mud
In the Kusumatalagarbha-vyuhalamkara-lokadhatu (Lotus Treasury World) depicted in the Avatamsaka Sutra, both the Saha world and the Pure Land of Ultimate Bliss seemingly reside on the 13th tier. Within the coordinates of the Dharma realm, these two lands are deeply interconnected and can easily overlap. Whether one can cross the vast distance between the ordinary and the sacred depends entirely on whether the practitioner's mind can resonate with the compassionate vows of Amida. Master Kumarajiva once used the metaphor of "plucking a lotus from foul mud," advising to take only the lotus and ignore the mud. This perhaps implies that while residing in this world of impurities, we should not cling to the surrounding filth, but rather focus on the lotus waiting to bloom within our own nature. When the mind's frequency aligns, the present Saha world might already be superimposed upon the pure Lotus Land of the 13th tier.
